Visualizing Competition & Collaboration

I’ve been enjoying zach lieberman’s sketches recently, and started thinking about similarly cell-based animations where cells interact dynamically with others in their neighborhood. I tried out a variety of competitive and collaborative dynamics for these interactions. The results, in retrospect, are reminiscent of evolutionary predator / prey simulations (like these) — I find the growing cityscape visualization sort of compelling (though no where near as aesthetically interesting as Zach’s sketches).

Rules / constraints for all experiments:

  • All cells are initialized to small random heights and small random rates of growth (positive and negative)
  • Simulation ends if all cells have no height, or any cell reaches 100 (and sometimes never)
  • Each cell’s dynamics are described completely by a single function that is passed the cell’s state, and all its neighbors, and returns a single change to its growth rate (can be up, down, or zero)
  • Cells are colored reddish when growth rate is decreasing (becoming more negative) and green when positive. Gray otherwise.
  • Neighborhood sizes can vary (here they’re between a radius of 1 and 5)

Each is labeled with a narrative of the rule used, and an interpretation of the result. Because of the random initializations, some of these look very different each time they’re run.

Takeaways:

  • In some cases it was surprisingly hard to turn a concept (my assumption of how a dynamic would work) into the expected result. Bad intuition for dynamical systems?
  • Many complex rules collapse quickly to trivial results — building in balance (to create stability) is sometimes challenging.
  • Flickering is a result of the logic getting stuck on the line between two states — a buffer or some implementation of momentum would help.
